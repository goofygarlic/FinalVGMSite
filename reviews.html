Building a Setting

To illustrate how music can build upon a setting, I will be drawing from multiple games from the Persona franchise. Specifically, I will be using moments from Persona 3 Reload and Persona 4 Golden. A key component in all 3 of these games is the interactions between characters of the main cast. These are seen through social links, a feature of the game where you can hang out with a character for the day.

One such example of a soundtrack that could play during a social link in Persona 4 is the track SMILE. In the social link for the character Rise Kujikawa, her rank 9 link uses this soundtrack in the background while she is talking to the player about her struggles.

Throughout not just her social link, but the main story as well, Rise’s development comes from finding her true self. At the start of the game, she is seen as someone with multiple personalities, none of whom truly encompass who she is. Progressing through her social link, she comes to learn that all these parts do in fact represent herself. The soundtrack SMILE enhances this moment using instruments such as the piano and synth. The overall happy tone of the song adds onto the moment as Rise has finally found a sense of her true self, giving her closure to one of her biggest insecurities. This soundtrack gives this moment a sense of closure and reflection to one of the most important characters in this game.

Another example can be seen in Persona 3 Reload, with the soundtrack Living With Determination – Iwatodai Dorm Arrangement. This soundtrack plays during the month of January, where at this point, the player is attempting to fight against the impending doom of the world, all culminating to the final day (January 31st). Iwatodai Dorm is where the main cast stays for the game.

The soundtrack samples Living With Determination, a soundtrack used in different moments earlier in the game to reflect a feeling of sorrow and sadness. The primary difference between the original and the Iwatodai Dorm arrangement is the use of drums and a lack of a violin, whereas the original does not contain any drums and uses a violin. This arrangement gives a sense of determination to the player, with the drums providing the feel to continue onward, even though the impending doom of the end of the world is coming soon. It still gives the same feeling of sorrow and dread the original does, with the added effect of trying to will yourself against it, which is the end goal of the entire game: to change your fate with your own power. The soundtrack delivers on this message in a beautiful way.

Enhancing Battles

Few series lean on musical tension as well as Pokémon Black 2 and White 2, and nowhere is that craft clearer than in the pair of battle themes that frame every gym challenge: the standard “Gym Leader Battle” and its crescendoing sibling, “Final Gym‑Leader Pokémon!”. Together they stage a miniature narrative of rising stakes, mirroring the player’s own adrenaline spike as the fight narrows to its final exchange.

The main Gym Leader theme greets you with a curt snare roll, then plunges into a syncopated, almost march‑like groove driven by slap bass and a propulsive 4‑on‑the‑floor kick. Underneath, Junichi Masuda threads a wiry electric‑guitar arpeggio that darts up the Mixolydian scale before resolving on a bright brass stab. It is equal parts stadium rock and Saturday‑morning‑anime, a shorthand for “you’ve earned the spotlight.” Crucially, the melody repeats in tidy sixteen‑bar loops, giving you just enough time to recognize its motifs before the arrangement subtly thickens: adding hi‑hat ghosts here, a counter‑melody in muted trumpet there. That evolving texture matches the pacing of an early‑battle setup: you’re trading damage, remembering abilities, tracking movesets. The track’s energy is determined but measured, like a coach shouting encouragement between innings.

Everything changes the moment the leader’s last Pokémon hits the field. A hi‑frequency sweep blows away the mix, and “Final Gym‑Leader Pokémon!” comes crashing in with distorted power chords, double‑time percussion, and—most memorable of all—a crowd sample clapping in frantic 2‑and‑4. The original bass groove reappears, but now it’s an octave lower and paired with dissonant brass clusters that lean heavily on minor‑second tension. Masuda and arranger Shota Kageyama effectively weaponize the listener’s familiarity: you recognize fragments from the first theme, yet every interval is sharpened, every cadence delayed. It’s as if the music itself realizes “the playbook is out the window; this is instinct.” Even the harmonic rhythm accelerates—key changes jump by major thirds, wrenching the tonal center upward and refusing the ear any place to settle.

Mechanically, this final theme arrives exactly when a Gym Leader tends to unveil a trump card: often a type‑coverage surprise or a speed EV spread that can sweep an unprepared team. The song’s relentless forward drive pressures you to act decisively; stall tactics feel psychologically wrong against that chanting crowd loop. And when you at last deliver the knockout, the track cuts to silence for a split‑second before the victory jingle, letting the weight of that last riff hang in the air like stadium reverb.

Taken together, these twin themes reinforce Unova’s competitive ethos: battles are sport, spectacle, and a test of will. Their clever escalation, from confident groove to white‑knuckle anthem, ensures every badge feels hard‑won, not just earned by level advantage but by nerves steeled in real time through music.
